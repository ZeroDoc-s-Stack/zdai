# Critical Analysis Skill

## Purpose

This skill applies a structured, non-biased rubric to any text. It finds:

- What the text is actually made of (vs. what it claims to be made of)
- Where it is logically or structurally strong or weak
- Where it breaks, and whether it knows it breaks
- What kind of person or world it assumes
- What reality it would produce if followed to its conclusion

The rubric has three layers that work together:

1. **Mode Detection** -- identifies what kind of text this is before analysis begins
2. **The 31 Questions** -- abstract diagnostic questions used as instruments within each stage
3. **The 9-Stage Track** -- a mode-specific process that organizes those questions

Always run Mode Detection first. Then run the 9-Stage Track for the detected mode in order. Each stage gates the next.

---

## Mode Detection

_Before running any stage, determine what kind of text this is._

**Goal:** Classify the text's primary mode. This determines which 9-stage track to use. Tell the reader what mode was detected and why, in 2--4 sentences. If the text mixes modes substantially, name the dominant mode and note the secondary mode -- then run the dominant mode's track while flagging where the secondary mode intrudes.

**The four modes:**

|Mode|Primary goal|Primary signal|
|---|---|---|
|**Narrative**|Truth through story -- character, event, arc, consequence|Plot structure, characters with agency, conflict and resolution, past-tense scene-setting|
|**Descriptive**|Accurate rendering of reality -- observation, sensory detail, scene|Sustained attention to particulars, absence of explicit argument, high sensory or spatial density|
|**Expository**|Transfer of understanding -- how something works, what something means|Definitions, process steps, causal chains, structured explanation without explicit persuasion|
|**Persuasive**|Change of belief or action -- claims, evidence, appeals, conclusions|Explicit thesis, evidence marshaled toward a conclusion, calls to believe or act|

**Mixed text guidance:** Most real texts blend modes. Apply this test: _what would be lost if you removed the dominant mode entirely?_ If removing the argument guts the text, it is Persuasive. If removing the story guts it, Narrative. If removing the explanation guts it, Expository. If removing the rendering guts it, Descriptive.

**Output of Mode Detection:**

- Detected mode (one word)
- Confidence (High / Medium / Mixed)
- 2--4 sentence rationale explaining the classification
- If Mixed: name the secondary mode and note where it appears

## Track A: Narrative

_Use when the text's primary mode is story._

Narrative texts embed their claims inside character, event, and consequence. They do not argue -- they demonstrate. The analysis does not evaluate the story as entertainment. It finds the moral logic, the worldview assumptions, and the reality the story constructs.

Run these nine stages in order.

---

### N-Stage 1: Moral Logic Extraction

_What is this story actually arguing, underneath the plot?_

**Goal:** Every narrative makes implicit claims about how the world works -- who deserves what, what actions lead to what outcomes, what human nature is. Strip the story and find those claims.

> **Q1.** What patterns in this story remain unchanged if translated into a different symbolic system -- a diagram, a mathematical structure, a different culture's telling? If the moral logic only works inside this story's specific framing, flag it.

> **Q2.** What claims survive if all sensory and emotional appeals are removed and only abstract structure remains? What is the skeleton?

> **Q3.** Which claims hold at multiple scales -- individual, community, civilization, species? A claim that only works at one scale is weaker than one that holds at all of them.

> **Q4.** If 90% of this text were false or biased, what would survive? Is that residue worth the whole text?

> **Q6.** What moral claims in this story require the fewest assumptions to hold? Are those the claims the story centers on, or are they buried under more culturally specific assertions?

**Output of this stage:**

- State the story's implicit moral thesis in one sentence (not a plot summary -- a claim about reality)
- List the cause-effect rules the story assumes (virtue leads to X; betrayal leads to Y; etc.)
- Note which of those rules are asserted by plot outcome vs. actually argued for
- Identify the story's irreducible moral minimum: what it is actually built on

**Signal of strength:** The story's moral logic is internally consistent, survives translation into other frames, and could be stated as a principle that holds outside the story.

**Signal of weakness:** The moral outcomes only work because the author controlled the plot. The story's "proof" is circular -- the conclusion was guaranteed by how the story was set up, and the moral logic dissolves when reframed.

---

### N-Stage 2: Thematic Unity Testing

_Does every element of the story serve the same underlying claim?_

**Goal:** A unified narrative compresses well -- all its parts, including subplots, minor characters, imagery, and setting, are doing work toward the same theme. A weak narrative has elements that contradict or ignore the central claim. This stage also tests whether the theme is one that independent traditions have arrived at independently -- a convergence signal that it tracks something real.

> **Q7.** What is the smallest set of principles this text requires to generate all its conclusions? Can you state them in one sentence each?

> **Q8.** Does this text reduce a large amount of observed reality with a small number of moving parts, or does it multiply explanations to cover exceptions?

> **Q9.** If this text had to be encoded in the shortest possible message without losing its essential claim, what would that message be? Is that message actually in the text, or did you have to construct it yourself?

> **Q10.** Where does this story's central moral pattern appear in unrelated traditions -- other cultures, other centuries, other domains -- that share none of its assumptions? Convergence without shared origin is a strength signal.

> **Q11.** What concepts in this story appear simultaneously in unrelated traditions with identical structure? The same moral logic appearing independently across cultures is evidence it tracks something real rather than something constructed.

> **Q12.** What has every serious treatment of this story's theme independently rediscovered -- and does this story include it? A narrative that omits what every other serious account of its subject has found is either incomplete or concealing something.

**Output of this stage:**

- State the story's theme in one sentence
- List every major element (character, subplot, setting, image) and note whether it supports, contradicts, or is neutral to that theme
- Flag elements that require separate explanation to fit the theme
- Note where the theme is independently corroborated across traditions, and where it diverges from all other serious accounts

**Signal of strength:** The theme is legible without being stated. Removing any major element would visibly damage it. The theme recurs independently across unrelated traditions.

**Signal of weakness:** The theme requires a foreword, afterword, or explicit statement to be visible. Elements work against each other. The story's moral pattern appears nowhere else, and the story does not argue for that divergence.

---

### N-Stage 3: Character Assumption Audit

_What kind of human being does this story treat as normal, default, or admirable?_

**Goal:** Every narrative centers certain kinds of people and marginalizes others. The protagonist's values, capabilities, and defaults are usually presented as the reader's point of orientation. Find what assumptions about human nature the story requires to function.

> **Q16.** What would this text mean to a reasoner with no emotions, no body, and no cultural history? What survives that removal?

> **Q18.** What claims in this text depend entirely on the observer sharing specific experiences, feelings, or cultural background? Are those claims presented as universal or particular?

> **Q5.** What in this text could a person rediscover alone, from scratch, with no access to this text or its tradition?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe the implicit "default human" the story centers
- List the values and capabilities the protagonist embodies that the story treats as aspirational or correct
- Note which characters exist primarily to be wrong, to suffer, or to serve the protagonist's arc -- and what that implies about who the story thinks matters

**Signal of strength:** The story's humanity is recognizable across cultures and centuries. Its characters hold genuine contradictions that are not resolved by the plot.

**Signal of weakness:** The story's characters only make sense inside a narrow cultural, historical, or ideological frame. The protagonist's perspective is treated as universal when it is particular.

---

### N-Stage 4: Plot Stress Testing

_Where does the story's moral logic break under pressure?_

**Goal:** Apply the story's own rules to cases it does not consider. Every narrative's moral logic has edges where it fails -- scenarios in which following the story's implied principles produces outcomes the story would not endorse.

> **Q13.** At what point do the text's own principles, followed consistently, produce a contradiction or an outcome the text does not endorse?

> **Q14.** What phenomena does this text consistently label paradox, exception, or mystery -- and do those labels cluster around a single unresolved problem?

> **Q15.** What is the most stable contradiction this text contains -- the one it keeps returning to without resolving?

> **Q24.** What would have to be true for this story's central moral claim to be false? Does the story engage that question, or does it construct a world where the question cannot arise? A moral logic that cannot specify what would defeat it is not a moral logic -- it is a preference protected from examination.

**Output of this stage:**

- Name the edge case where the story's moral rules produce a wrong answer
- Name the most stable unresolved tension in the narrative (not plot tension -- thematic tension)
- State what would falsify the story's central moral claim
- State what kind of story would have to be told to resolve what this story leaves open

**Signal of strength:** The story acknowledges its own moral complexity. Its resolution is earned, not imposed. It can specify the conditions under which its moral logic would fail.

**Signal of weakness:** The story resolves by authorial fiat -- coincidence, unexplained change of heart, or villain incompetence. The moral logic only works because the author prevented the cases that would break it. It cannot state what would defeat it.

---

### N-Stage 5: Vantage Point Testing

_Whose eyes does this story require you to see through, and what does that hide?_

**Goal:** Every narrative has a vantage point -- a perspective that is privileged, trusted, and treated as normal. That vantage point determines what is visible and what is invisible in the story. Find whose experience is structurally excluded.

> **Q16.** What would this text mean to a reasoner with no emotions, no body, and no cultural history?

> **Q17.** What would a mind without survival pressure -- no fear of death, no scarcity -- still find true in this text?

> **Q18.** What claims in this text depend entirely on the observer sharing specific experiences, feelings, or cultural background?

**Output of this stage:**

- Name the vantage point the story privileges
- Describe what the story looks like from the perspective of its most marginal or antagonized character
- List what is structurally invisible from the story's primary vantage point

**Signal of strength:** The story's vantage point is conscious of its own limits. Antagonists have coherent inner lives. The protagonist is not always right.

**Signal of weakness:** The story's vantage point treats one perspective as objective reality. Characters outside the protagonist's frame exist only as functions of the plot.

---

### N-Stage 6: Projected World Testing

_What kind of reality does this story build if taken as a model for how life works?_

**Goal:** Stories teach. Readers absorb their moral logic as a model of reality even when they know the story is fiction. Project what world a reader constructs if they internalize this story's rules as true. Then test whether that world is durable, humane, and self-correcting -- or fragile, coercive, and self-sealing.

> **Q19.** What ideas in this text have remained stable across the longest time spans? Which moral claims resemble attractors that every culture independently rediscovers -- and which are recent, specific, or likely to be revised?

> **Q20.** Which of this story's moral claims are most likely to survive long scrutiny, based on how similar claims have fared historically? What resembles the golden rule (rediscovered independently, millennia-stable) and what resembles a morality tied to a specific historical moment?

> **Q21.** Which moral claims in this story become stronger the more carefully they are examined -- and which depend on not being examined too closely?

> **Q22.** What method of discovering moral truth does this story advocate or model -- lived experience, revelation, reason, tradition, suffering, relationship? Is that method one with a strong track record, or does it produce conclusions that cannot be tested or revised?

> **Q23.** Does this story arrive at its moral conclusions through contradiction (exposing what is wrong), symmetry (finding what is fair), or optimization (finding what produces the best outcomes)? Is that method appropriate to the moral problem the story is actually solving?

> **Q25.** What conclusions persist after recursively removing all assumptions, interpretations, and metaphors from this text?

> **Q26.** What truths remain when all motivations embedded in this text (fear, desire, survival, tribal identity) are treated as noise?

> **Q27.** What does this story's moral logic share with what humans consistently rebuild after losing everything -- justice as reciprocity, care for the vulnerable, narrative itself? Claims that align with these deep attractors have the most durable grounding.

> **Q28.** What direction does this story push its reader -- toward greater complexity and self-awareness, or toward simpler categories and less self-examination? Does it increase or decrease the specialness it assigns to its in-group?

> **Q29.** What kind of moral answer does this story ultimately point toward -- a brute fact about human nature, a necessary structure, a transcendent source, a process of becoming? Does it name that destination honestly, or does it claim the journey has no destination?

> **Q30.** If this story's moral model is an approximation of something real about human nature or the good life, what is the shape of the thing it is approximating? Does the story know? Does it say?

> **Q31.** If all human moral intuition is assumed to be fundamentally distorted by self-interest, survival pressure, and cultural bias, does this story have the resources to correct for that -- or does it depend on the very intuitions it cannot fully trust?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe the world a reader inhabits if they fully internalize this story's moral logic
- Note whether that world is stable, humane, and coherent -- or brittle, coercive, and self-sealing
- Identify the story's asymptotic destination: what kind of person does it try to produce?
- Note which moral claims are durably grounded and which are fragile or culturally contingent
- State whether the story gives readers tools to question its own moral model, or seals them inside it

**Signal of strength:** The world the story builds is more coherent and humane than the one it depicts. Its moral claims align with durable human patterns and become stronger under examination. It gives readers tools to revise its own conclusions.

**Signal of weakness:** The world the story builds requires enemies to function, depends on the reader remaining emotionally activated, or collapses when its premises are removed. Its moral claims require the reader not to look too closely.

---

### N-Stage 7: Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case that this story's moral logic is wrong?_

**Goal:** Construct the most damaging possible critique of the story's central moral claim, sourced from a framework that shares none of its assumptions. This is not a critique of the prose or the plot -- it is a critique of the embedded argument.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Identify the story's single most load-bearing moral claim.
2. Find the strongest critic of that claim from a completely independent tradition (different domain, culture, or century).
3. State their case in its strongest form, as if writing their argument, not summarizing it.
4. Note what the story would have to change to survive that critique.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage produces only mild objections the story already anticipates, the adversarial force was not applied hard enough. Re-run it.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest external case against the story's moral logic, stated fully
- What the story lacks to answer it
- Whether the gap is fatal or bridgeable

---

### N-Stage 8: Anti-Adversarial Force

_What independent evidence corroborates this story's moral logic?_

**Goal:** Find the most compelling independent corroboration of the story's central moral claim, from a framework that shares none of its assumptions.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Find a thinker, tradition, or body of evidence that arrives at the same moral conclusion without having read this story or shared its premises.
2. State that corroboration in its strongest form.
3. Note what it adds that the story itself did not supply.

**Anti-drift check:** The corroboration should be genuinely surprising -- something the story's author might not have anticipated.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest independent corroboration of the story's moral logic
- What it adds that the story itself did not supply
- Whether the convergence is structural or merely superficial

---

### N-Stage 9: Neutral Synthesis

_What does an observer with no stake in either outcome conclude?_

**Goal:** After adversarial and anti-adversarial forces have both been applied at full strength, step back. Do not average them. Ask what a reasoner with no prior commitment to the story's success or failure would conclude.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. State what N-7 and N-8 together established.
2. Identify which moral claims survived both forces intact, which were weakened, and which were strengthened.
3. State the net assessment without hedging.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage sounds like a polished endorsement of the story, or a polished dismissal of it, the neutral force was not applied. Neutral does not mean kind. It means unowned.

**Output of this stage:**

- Moral claims that survived both forces: genuinely robust
- Claims weakened by N-7 and not recovered by N-8: acknowledged as vulnerable
- Claims unexpectedly strengthened by N-8: noted as underargued assets
- A one-sentence net assessment that neither the author nor the story's strongest critic would fully endorse

## Track B: Descriptive

_Use when the text's primary mode is rendering -- observation, scene, sensory detail._

Descriptive texts do not argue openly. They select, frame, and render. The analysis finds what the selection implies, whose vantage point is embedded in the framing, and what the rendering conceals by what it does not show.

Run these nine stages in order.

---

### D-Stage 1: Selection Audit

_What did this text choose to notice, and what does that choosing imply?_

**Goal:** Every act of description is an act of selection. What is included, foregrounded, and named is a set of implicit claims about what matters. Find those claims.

> **Q2.** What claims survive if all sensory and emotional appeals are removed and only abstract structure remains?

> **Q4.** If 90% of this text were false or biased, what would survive?

> **Q5.** What in this text could any observer rediscover independently, from scratch, with no access to this text or its cultural context? Selections that any careful observer would make carry more weight than selections that only this observer's position makes visible.

> **Q6.** What claims require the fewest assumptions to hold? Are those the claims the text centers on, or buried?

**Output of this stage:**

- List what the text foregrounds (spends the most language on)
- List what the text backgrounds or omits entirely
- State the implicit claim that the selection pattern suggests
- Note which selections any observer would likely make, and which are specific to this observer's position

**Signal of strength:** The selection is conscious of its own partiality. The text acknowledges what it is not showing. Its most prominent selections are ones multiple independent observers would share.

**Signal of weakness:** The text presents its selection as neutral observation. It implies completeness while practicing omission. Its selections are position-specific but presented as universal.

---

### D-Stage 2: Framing Analysis

_How does the text's structure shape what the reader sees before they begin reading?_

**Goal:** Framing is the container the description is poured into. Titles, sequence, opening and closing images, and the order of detail all prime the reader to interpret what follows in a particular way. Find the frame and examine what it does.

> **Q1.** What patterns in this text remain unchanged if translated into a different symbolic system? If the description only makes sense inside its original frame, that frame is doing heavy lifting.

> **Q7.** What is the smallest set of principles this text requires to generate all its conclusions?

> **Q8.** Does this text render the subject with a small number of well-chosen details that do a lot of work, or does it multiply detail to cover gaps in coherence? Efficient framing does more with less; a frame that needs many moving parts to hold together is protecting something.

> **Q9.** If this text had to be encoded in the shortest possible message without losing its essential claim, what would that message be?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe the frame: what interpretive instructions does the text give before the description begins?
- State what the description would mean if the frame were removed or reversed
- Note whether the frame is doing work the description's content cannot do on its own
- Note whether the frame is efficient (a few structural choices doing real work) or padded (complexity protecting a fragile interpretation)

**Signal of strength:** The frame and the content are consistent. The content could stand without the frame. The framing is economical.

**Signal of weakness:** The frame is carrying the meaning. The description, reframed, would support a different or opposite conclusion. The framing requires many auxiliary choices to hold the interpretation in place.

---

### D-Stage 3: Vantage Point Identification

_Whose eyes is this description written through, and what does that position make visible or invisible?_

**Goal:** Every description assumes a position in space, time, culture, and social role. That position determines what can be seen and what cannot. Find the vantage point, map its limits, and test whether the rendering it produces is one that independent observers would converge on or diverge from.

> **Q10.** Where do independent observers of the same subject -- from unrelated cultures, disciplines, or centuries -- converge on the same features this text foregrounds? Convergence without shared assumptions is a signal the selection is tracking something real.

> **Q11.** What aspects of this subject appear across unrelated descriptions with identical structure, suggesting they are features of the thing rather than artifacts of the observer?

> **Q12.** What has every serious attempt to describe this subject or category of subject independently noticed -- and does this text include it? A description that omits what every other account finds is either unusual or concealing something.

> **Q16.** What would this text mean to a reasoner with no emotions, no body, and no cultural history?

> **Q17.** What would a mind without survival pressure still find true in this text?

> **Q18.** What claims in this text depend entirely on the observer sharing specific experiences, feelings, or cultural background?

**Output of this stage:**

- Name the vantage point: where is the observer standing (physically, socially, culturally, temporally)?
- List what is invisible from that vantage point
- Note whether the text acknowledges its positional limits or presents its view as neutral
- Note which features of the rendering are independently corroborated and which are vantage-point-specific

**Signal of strength:** The text is aware of its own position. It does not claim to see everything. Its most prominent features are ones independent observers would converge on.

**Signal of weakness:** The text presents a positioned view as neutral observation. It treats its vantage point as the only rational one. Its selections diverge from all other serious accounts without argument.

---

### D-Stage 4: Omission Stress Testing

_What would a description from a different position reveal that this one conceals?_

**Goal:** The most important thing a description does is decide what not to show. Apply pressure to those omissions. Find what a reasonably different observer would have described instead, and what that reveals about this description's implicit agenda.

> **Q13.** At what point do the text's own selection principles, followed consistently, produce a contradiction or an outcome the text does not endorse?

> **Q14.** What does this text consistently label as too complex, too subjective, or too contested to render -- and do those labels cluster around a single feature of the subject the text cannot face directly?

> **Q15.** What is the most stable tension this text contains -- the feature of the subject it keeps gesturing toward without fully showing?

> **Q24.** What would have to be true for this text's implicit framing to be wrong? Does the description engage that possibility, or is it structured to make the question unaskable?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe what a description of the same subject from a different vantage point would foreground
- Name the most important thing this description does not show
- Name the feature the text most consistently avoids rendering directly
- State whether those omissions are defensible (the text cannot show everything) or strategic (the omissions protect an implicit claim)

**Signal of strength:** The omissions are defensible. The text is doing what any description must: choosing. It does not suppress inconvenient particulars.

**Signal of weakness:** The omissions cluster around a specific interest or conclusion. What is not shown would change the meaning of what is shown. The text cannot state what would make its framing wrong.

---

### D-Stage 5: Language Precision Testing

_Is the language doing what description requires, or is it smuggling judgment into observation?_

**Goal:** Description requires precision. But descriptive language is never neutral -- word choice, metaphor, and tone carry evaluative content. Find where the text crosses from rendering into judging, and whether it knows it is doing so.

> **Q2.** What claims survive if all sensory and emotional appeals are removed?

> **Q25.** What conclusions persist after recursively removing all assumptions, interpretations, and metaphors?

> **Q26.** What truths remain when all motivations embedded in this text are treated as noise?

**Output of this stage:**

- List the five most evaluatively loaded words or phrases in the text
- For each: state what the text would mean if a neutral synonym replaced it
- Note whether the loaded language is consistent with the text's implicit vantage point, or inconsistent (which would suggest internal conflict)

**Signal of strength:** The text's evaluative language is visible and consistent. It does not pretend to neutrality it does not have.

**Signal of weakness:** The text claims to be neutral observation while using language that carries strong judgment. The evaluation is smuggled inside the rendering.

---

### D-Stage 6: Completeness Testing

_What would a complete description of this subject require that this text does not provide?_

**Goal:** Project what the subject actually is, independent of this description. Then measure the gap between the subject and the rendering. A strong description acknowledges the gap. A weak one mistakes the rendering for the thing. This stage also tests the durability of the choices made: are they the kind that get stronger under scrutiny, or weaker?

> **Q3.** Which features of the subject hold at multiple scales -- and does this description capture them at the right scale?

> **Q19.** Which of this description's choices have remained stable across independent accounts of the same subject over time? Features that every serious observer notices across centuries are more likely to be features of the thing than of the observer.

> **Q20.** Which choices in this description are most likely to survive long scrutiny -- and which resemble the confident selections of an era rather than durable observations?

> **Q21.** Which features rendered in this description become more clearly correct the more carefully the subject is examined -- and which depend on not being examined too closely?

> **Q22.** What method of observation does this description use -- direct sensory contact, mediated report, archival reconstruction, inference? Is that method one with a strong track record for this kind of subject?

> **Q23.** Does this description find its most important features through contrast (what stands out), pattern (what repeats), or refinement (what remains under close attention)? Is that method appropriate for what it is trying to render?

> **Q27.** What features of this subject would any observer eventually notice, regardless of cultural position or prior knowledge -- and does this description include them?

> **Q28.** What direction does this description push the reader's understanding -- toward greater particularity or greater generality, toward the subject's uniqueness or its typicality? Is that direction honest about what it is doing?

> **Q29.** What is this description ultimately pointing toward -- a feeling, a judgment, a fact, a relationship? Does it know its own destination, or does it present itself as having no destination?

> **Q30.** If this description is an approximation of the subject, what is the shape of the thing it is approximating? Does the text acknowledge the gap between the rendering and the thing, or does it present the rendering as the thing itself?

> **Q31.** If all human perception is assumed to be fundamentally shaped by cultural position, embodiment, and prior expectation, does this description have the resources to correct for that -- or does it depend on the very perception it cannot fully trust?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe the subject as it would appear to three genuinely different observers
- Note what each observer would see that this text does not show
- State whether the gap between this description and a complete one is small (the text is honest) or large (the text is selective in ways that matter)
- Note which of the text's choices are durably grounded and which are fragile or era-specific
- State what the description is ultimately pointing toward, and whether it knows it

**Signal of strength:** The description is explicitly partial. It does not claim to exhaust the subject. Its most prominent choices are ones that become more clearly correct under examination.

**Signal of weakness:** The description implies it has shown the subject as it is. The gap between the rendering and the thing is large and unacknowledged. Its choices depend on the reader not looking too closely or from a different position.

---

### D-Stage 7: Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case that this description is not neutral observation but a positioned argument?_

**Goal:** Construct the most damaging possible critique of the text's claim to be rendering rather than arguing. The adversarial case here is not that the text is wrong -- it is that the text is doing something other than what it presents itself as doing.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Identify the implicit claim the description's selections, framing, and language add up to.
2. Find a description of the same subject from a completely different vantage point that arrives at a structurally different rendering.
3. State the case that this text's "description" is actually argument-by-selection.
4. Note what the text would have to add or change to become genuinely neutral.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage produces only mild observations the text already acknowledges, the adversarial force was not applied hard enough.

**Output of this stage:**

- The implicit argument the description is making, stated as a thesis
- The strongest case that this rendering is not neutral
- Whether that gap is fatal to the text's credibility or merely honest partiality

---

### D-Stage 8: Anti-Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case that this description is doing what description should do?_

**Goal:** Apply the same rigor in the opposite direction. Find the most compelling case that the text's selection, framing, and language are appropriate -- that a different vantage point would add to this description rather than replace it.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Find an independent account of the same or similar subject that corroborates this text's framing.
2. State why that convergence suggests the selection is tracking something real rather than merely reflecting the observer's bias.
3. Note what it adds to the text's credibility that the text itself did not supply.

**Anti-drift check:** The corroboration should be genuinely independent. If it shares the text's cultural or positional assumptions, it does not count.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest independent corroboration of this text's rendering
- What it adds that the text itself did not supply
- Whether the convergence is structural or merely superficial

---

### D-Stage 9: Neutral Synthesis

_What does an observer with no stake in either outcome conclude?_

**Goal:** After adversarial and anti-adversarial forces have both been applied, step back. Ask what a reasoner with no prior commitment to this description's validity or its critique would conclude.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. State what D-7 and D-8 together established.
2. Identify which elements of the description are honest rendering, which are honest selection, and which are implicit argument.
3. State the net assessment without hedging.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage sounds like a defense of the text or a demolition of it, the neutral force was not applied.

**Output of this stage:**

- Elements that survived both forces as genuine rendering: acknowledged as such
- Elements weakened by D-7 and not recovered by D-8: identified as implicit argument masquerading as description
- A one-sentence net assessment of the text's honesty as a descriptive act

---

## Track C: Expository

_Use when the text's primary mode is explanation -- how something works, what something means, the mechanics of a process or concept._

Expository texts make implicit claims constantly: that their model of the topic is correct, complete, and the right level of abstraction. The analysis tests whether the explanation is actually accurate, efficient, and honest about its own limits -- or whether it is internally consistent while being subtly wrong, outdated, or framed to favor a particular conclusion.

Run these nine stages in order.

---

### E-Stage 1: Model Identification

_What model of reality is this explanation built on, and what does that model assume?_

**Goal:** Every explanation is built on a model -- a set of assumptions about what the subject is, how it works, and what level of abstraction is appropriate. Find that model and make its assumptions explicit before evaluating the explanation.

> **Q1.** What patterns in this text remain unchanged if translated into a different symbolic system? If it only works in its original framing, flag it.

> **Q2.** What claims survive if all sensory and emotional appeals are removed and only abstract structure remains?

> **Q4.** If 90% of this text were false or biased, what would survive? What is the model's irreducible residue?

> **Q5.** What in this text could a person rediscover alone, from scratch, with no access to this text or its tradition? Claims that align with independently rediscoverable knowledge have deeper grounding; claims that require cultural transmission to hold carry a heavier explanatory burden.

> **Q6.** What claims require the fewest assumptions to hold?

**Output of this stage:**

- State the model the explanation is built on in one sentence
- List the minimum assumptions the model requires
- Note whether those assumptions are stated explicitly or embedded invisibly
- Identify which assumptions could be independently rediscovered and which must be inherited

**Signal of strength:** The model's assumptions are named. The explanation knows what it is taking for granted, and its core survives aggressive stripping.

**Signal of weakness:** The model's assumptions are invisible. The explanation presents its framework as the only rational way to understand the subject, and its residue after stripping is thinner than it appeared.

---

### E-Stage 2: Compression Testing

_Is this explanation using the fewest moving parts that account for the most?_

**Goal:** A strong explanation compresses well -- it covers a wide range of cases with a small number of principles. A weak explanation multiplies concepts, exceptions, and auxiliary rules to protect its model from cases that do not fit.

> **Q7.** What is the smallest set of principles this text requires to generate all its conclusions?

> **Q8.** Does this text reduce a large amount of observed reality with a small number of moving parts, or does it multiply explanations to cover exceptions?

> **Q9.** If this text had to be encoded in the shortest possible message without losing its essential claim, what would that message be?

**Output of this stage:**

- State the explanation's core mechanism in one sentence
- List every auxiliary concept or exception and note whether it is load-bearing or protective
- Flag every place complexity is added to protect the model rather than explain the subject

**Signal of strength:** The explanation's core can be stated simply, and the complexity it adds is genuinely explanatory.

**Signal of weakness:** You can compress the explanation better than it compresses itself. Complexity is being used to hide the places where the model does not fit.

---

### E-Stage 3: Accuracy and Convergence Testing

_Does this explanation agree with how other serious frameworks model the same subject?_

**Goal:** Find where the explanation's model is corroborated by independent accounts of the same subject, and where it diverges. Divergence is not automatically wrong -- but it requires explicit argument, not silent assertion.

> **Q10.** Where do the claims of this text appear in unrelated domains that share no assumptions with this text?

> **Q11.** What concepts in this text appear simultaneously in unrelated traditions with identical structure?

> **Q12.** What has every serious attempt to formalize this domain independently rediscovered -- and does this text include it?

**Output of this stage:**

- List where the explanation is corroborated by independent accounts
- List where the explanation diverges from other serious frameworks
- For every divergence: note whether the text argues for it or simply asserts it

**Signal of strength:** The explanation includes what every serious account of the subject has independently found. Where it diverges, it says so and argues for the divergence.

**Signal of weakness:** The explanation silently diverges from other accounts without acknowledging the divergence. It presents its model as settled when it is contested.

---

### E-Stage 4: Edge Case Stress Testing

_Where does this explanation break down?_

**Goal:** Every model has edges where it fails. Find the cases the explanation cannot account for, the exceptions it labels without explaining, and the phenomena that its model predicts wrong.

> **Q13.** At what point do the text's own principles, followed consistently, produce a contradiction or an outcome the text does not endorse?

> **Q14.** What phenomena does this text consistently label paradox, exception, or mystery?

> **Q15.** What is the most stable contradiction this text contains -- the one it keeps returning to without resolving? Is that contradiction acknowledged or papered over?

> **Q23.** Does this text produce truth more often through identifying contradictions, through symmetry, or through optimization? Is that method appropriate to the stage of inquiry the text is at, or does it reveal a mismatch between the method and the problem?

> **Q24.** What would have to be true for this text's central claim to be false?

**Output of this stage:**

- Name the edge case where the explanation's model produces the wrong answer
- Name the most stable unresolved contradiction the explanation contains
- Name the most important exception the explanation labels without explaining
- State what evidence or case would falsify the explanation's central model
- Note whether the explanation's method of generating truth (contradiction, symmetry, or optimization) fits the problem it is solving

**Signal of strength:** The explanation names its own limits. It says explicitly where the model applies and where it does not.

**Signal of weakness:** The explanation presents its model as universal while quietly excluding the cases that break it. It cannot state what would defeat it.

---

### E-Stage 5: Abstraction Level Testing

_Is this explanation pitched at the right level of abstraction for what it is claiming to explain?_

**Goal:** Explanations can fail by being too abstract (true but useless) or too concrete (accurate for this case but not generalizable). Find whether the explanation's level of abstraction is appropriate to its stated goal.

> **Q3.** Which claims hold at multiple scales -- individual, community, civilization, species?

> **Q16.** What would this text mean to a reasoner with no emotions, no body, and no cultural history? What survives that removal -- and what depends on a situated, embodied reader to seem true?

> **Q17.** What would a mind without survival pressure -- no fear of death, no scarcity -- still find true in this text? What is purely structural vs. what is motivated by the human condition?

> **Q18.** What claims in this text depend entirely on the observer sharing specific experiences, feelings, or cultural background? Are those claims presented as universal or particular?

> **Q28.** What direction does this text push knowledge -- toward unity, complexity, abstraction, particularity?

> **Q30.** If all the models in this text are approximations, what is the shape of the thing they are approximating?

**Output of this stage:**

- State the level of abstraction the explanation operates at
- Note whether that level is appropriate: does it generalize enough to be useful, and stay concrete enough to be testable?
- Separate claims that hold for any observer from claims that require a culturally or emotionally situated reader
- Flag any place the explanation shifts abstraction level without acknowledging the shift, or presents observer-dependent claims as universal

**Signal of strength:** The explanation is explicit about what level it operates at, what that level cannot reach, and which of its claims require a specific kind of reader.

**Signal of weakness:** The explanation implies generality it has not earned, or implies precision it cannot achieve. It conflates the map with the territory, or presents culturally grounded claims as accessible to any rational observer.

---

### E-Stage 6: Projected Decisions Testing

_What decisions or beliefs does this explanation produce in a reader who fully accepts it?_

**Goal:** Explanations have consequences. A reader who internalizes this model will make different decisions than one who does not. Project those decisions and evaluate whether they are better or worse than the decisions a different model would produce.

> **Q19.** What ideas in this text have remained stable across the longest time spans despite changes in worldview? Which claims resemble attractors that every serious account of the subject rediscovers?

> **Q20.** Which claims in this text are most likely to survive long scrutiny, based on how similar claims have fared historically? What resembles thermodynamics (refined by measurement, never overturned at core) -- and what resembles early nutrition science (confident, likely to be revised)?

> **Q21.** What truths in this text became stronger as measurement and evidence improved, rather than being replaced? Which claims predict that better measurement will confirm them -- and which require measurement to stay imprecise?

> **Q22.** Does this text produce its explanation through controlled analysis, mathematical derivation, adversarial testing -- or through authority without independent verification? Does it try to be wrong before claiming to be right?

> **Q25.** What conclusions persist after recursively removing all assumptions, interpretations, and metaphors?

> **Q26.** What truths remain when all motivations embedded in this text (the author's purpose, audience assumptions, disciplinary conventions) are treated as noise?

> **Q27.** What does this text share with what humans consistently rebuild after losing knowledge? Claims that align with independently rediscovered knowledge have the deepest grounding.

> **Q29.** What kind of answer does this text trend toward as its ultimate explanation -- a brute fact, a necessary structure, a process, a person? Does the text name its asymptotic destination honestly, or does it claim to have escaped the explanatory regress?

> **Q31.** If all human cognition is assumed to be fundamentally misaligned with reality, does this text have the resources to correct for that?

**Output of this stage:**

- List the decisions a reader will make differently after accepting this explanation
- Note whether those decisions are better calibrated to reality than what the reader would have done without the explanation
- Identify whether the explanation is self-correcting (it gives the reader tools to update it) or self-sealing (it gives the reader reasons to protect it from revision)
- Note which claims resemble durable attractors vs. fragile, culturally contingent artifacts
- State what the explanation's asymptotic destination is, and whether it knows it

**Signal of strength:** The explanation makes the reader more accurate and gives them tools to update the model when evidence changes.

**Signal of weakness:** The explanation makes the reader more confident without making them more accurate. It produces self-sealing beliefs resistant to revision.

---

### E-Stage 7: Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case that this explanation is wrong or misleading?_

**Goal:** Construct the most damaging possible critique of the explanation's central model, sourced from a rival framework that arrives at a different account of the same subject.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Identify the explanation's single most load-bearing claim -- the mechanism everything else depends on.
2. Find the strongest rival framework that accounts for the same phenomena differently.
3. State the rival account in its strongest form, as if writing its argument, not summarizing it.
4. Note what the explanation would have to add or change to survive that critique.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage produces only mild objections the explanation already anticipates, the adversarial force was not applied hard enough.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest rival account of the same subject
- What the explanation lacks to answer it
- Whether the gap is fatal or bridgeable

---

### E-Stage 8: Anti-Adversarial Force

_What independent evidence most strongly corroborates this explanation's model?_

**Goal:** Find the most compelling independent corroboration of the explanation's central model, from a domain or tradition that shares none of its assumptions.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Find a domain or tradition that independently arrives at the same model or mechanism without having borrowed it from this text.
2. State that corroboration in its strongest form.
3. Note what it adds that the explanation itself did not supply.

**Anti-drift check:** The corroboration should be genuinely surprising. Convergence from a domain with shared assumptions does not count.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest independent corroboration of this explanation's model
- What it adds that the text itself did not supply
- Whether the convergence is structural or superficial

---

### E-Stage 9: Neutral Synthesis

_What does an observer with no stake in either outcome conclude?_

**Goal:** After adversarial and anti-adversarial forces have both been applied, step back. Ask what a reasoner with no prior commitment to this explanation's accuracy or its critique would conclude.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. State what E-7 and E-8 together established.
2. Identify which claims survived both forces intact, which were weakened, and which were strengthened.
3. State the net assessment without hedging.

**Anti-drift check:** Neutral does not mean splitting the difference. It means owning neither the text nor its critique.

**Output of this stage:**

- Claims that survived both forces: genuinely robust
- Claims weakened by E-7 and not recovered by E-8: acknowledged as vulnerable
- Claims unexpectedly strengthened by E-8: noted as underargued assets
- A one-sentence net assessment of the explanation's reliability as a model of its subject

---

## Track D: Persuasive

_Use when the text's primary mode is argument -- claims marshaled toward a conclusion, with intent to change belief or action._

Persuasive texts are the most honest about what they are doing. They want to win. The analysis tests whether they deserve to -- whether the argument holds up when its rhetoric is stripped, its failure points are pressed, and its strongest critics are given a full hearing.

Run these nine stages in order.

---

### P-Stage 1: Invariance and Appeal Audit

_What is this text actually arguing, and how is it making its case?_

**Goal:** Find the skeleton. What is the text actually arguing when stripped of rhetoric, emotional appeals, and cultural framing? Then audit the types of appeals being used -- because persuasive texts routinely substitute emotional or authority-based appeals for logical ones, and the rubric must catch that.

> **Q1.** What patterns in this text remain unchanged if translated into a different symbolic system? If it only works in its original framing, flag it.

> **Q2.** What claims survive if all sensory and emotional appeals are removed and only abstract structure remains?

> **Q3.** Which claims hold at multiple scales -- individual, community, civilization, species? A claim that only works at one scale is weaker than one that holds at all of them.

> **Q4.** If 90% of this text were false or biased, what would survive?

> **Q5.** What in this text could a person rediscover alone, from scratch, with no access to this text or its tradition? Claims that align with independently rediscoverable beliefs have deeper grounding; claims that contradict them carry a heavier explanatory burden.

> **Q6.** What claims require the fewest assumptions to hold?

**Appeal audit -- for each major claim, identify the primary appeal type:**

- _Logos_ (logic and evidence): the claim is supported by reasoning or data
- _Ethos_ (authority and credibility): the claim is supported by the speaker's or a cited authority's standing
- _Pathos_ (emotion and identity): the claim is supported by emotional resonance or group membership

**Output of this stage:**

- List the text's core claims, stripped of rhetoric
- For each claim: note which appeal type is doing the primary work
- Flag every place a pathos or ethos appeal is substituting for a logos argument
- Identify the text's irreducible minimum: what it is actually arguing

**Signal of strength:** The text's core claims survive the removal of emotional and authority appeals. Its rhetoric amplifies its logic rather than replacing it.

**Signal of weakness:** The text's core claims depend on emotional activation or deference to authority. Stripped of those, the argument weakens or disappears.

---

### P-Stage 2: Compression Testing

_Is this text saying the most with the least, or the least with the most?_

**Goal:** Measure the text's argumentative efficiency. A strong argument compresses well -- its core can be stated simply, and complexity serves the argument. A weak argument multiplies premises and qualifications to protect claims that do not hold under simpler scrutiny.

> **Q7.** What is the smallest set of principles this text requires to generate all its conclusions?

> **Q8.** Does this text reduce a large amount of observed reality with a small number of moving parts, or does it multiply explanations to cover exceptions?

> **Q9.** If this text had to be encoded in the shortest possible message without losing its essential claim, what would that message be?

**Output of this stage:**

- State the text's central argument in one sentence
- List the minimum premises required to generate all conclusions
- Flag every place the text adds complexity to protect a claim rather than support it

**Signal of strength:** The text's compression is already visible inside it. The author knew what they were arguing.

**Signal of weakness:** You can compress the text better than the text compresses itself. Complexity is hiding weak premises.

---

### P-Stage 3: Convergence Testing

_Does this argument arrive where other serious thinking arrives, independently?_

**Goal:** Find where the text's claims are corroborated by independent lines of reasoning, and where they diverge from all other serious frameworks.

> **Q10.** Where do the claims of this text appear in unrelated domains that share no assumptions with this text?

> **Q11.** What concepts in this text appear simultaneously in unrelated traditions with identical structure?

> **Q12.** What has every serious attempt to formalize this domain independently rediscovered -- and does this text include it?

**Output of this stage:**

- List where the text's claims are independently corroborated
- List where the text diverges from all other serious frameworks
- For every divergence: note whether the text argues for it or simply asserts it

**Signal of strength:** Claims that appear independently across unrelated traditions are tracking something real.

**Signal of weakness:** A text that diverges widely and argues minimally is making a large bet with thin collateral.

---

### P-Stage 4: Stress Testing

_Where does this argument break, and what does the breaking tell us?_

**Goal:** Find the argument's failure points. Every serious argument breaks somewhere. The question is whether it knows where and says so honestly.

> **Q13.** At what point do the text's own principles, followed consistently, produce a contradiction or an outcome the text does not endorse?

> **Q14.** What phenomena does this text consistently label paradox, exception, or mystery?

> **Q15.** What is the most stable contradiction this text contains?

> **Q24.** What would have to be true for this text's central claim to be false?

**Output of this stage:**

- Name the text's primary failure point
- Name the most stable unresolved contradiction
- State clearly what would falsify the text's central claim
- Note whether the text acknowledges its failure points or conceals them

**Signal of strength:** The text names its own limits honestly.

**Signal of weakness:** The text labels its breaks as mystery or exception without engaging them. It cannot state what would defeat it.

---

### P-Stage 5: Observer Dependency Testing

_What kind of person does this argument require to seem convincing?_

**Goal:** Find the hidden assumptions about the reader embedded in the argument. An argument that presents observer-dependent claims as universal truths is making an error it may not know it is making.

> **Q16.** What would this text mean to a reasoner with no emotions, no body, and no cultural history?

> **Q17.** What would a mind without survival pressure still find true in this text?

> **Q18.** What claims in this text depend entirely on the observer sharing specific experiences, feelings, or cultural background?

**Output of this stage:**

- Separate the text's universal claims from its observer-dependent claims
- Flag every place an observer-dependent claim is presented as universal
- Note what the text's implicit audience actually is vs. what it claims its audience is

**Signal of strength:** The text honestly marks which claims are particular and which are universal.

**Signal of weakness:** The text presents culturally or emotionally dependent claims as self-evident to any reasonable person.

---

### P-Stage 6: Projected Reality Testing

_What world does this argument produce if followed all the way through?_

**Goal:** Project the world the text would create if its argument were accepted and acted on universally. An argument that produces a coherent, stable, humane, and self-correcting reality is stronger than one that produces a fragile, coercive, or self-sealing one.

> **Q19.** What ideas in this text have remained stable across the longest time spans despite changes in worldview? Which claims resemble attractors that every civilization rediscovers -- and which are recent, culturally specific, or likely to be revised?

> **Q20.** Which claims in this text are most likely to survive long scrutiny, based on how similar claims have fared historically? What in it resembles thermodynamics (robust, refined by measurement, never overturned at core), and what resembles early nutrition science (confident, culturally motivated, likely to be revised)?

> **Q21.** What truths in this text became stronger as measurement and evidence improved, rather than being replaced? Which claims predict that better measurement will confirm them -- and which require measurement to stay imprecise?

> **Q22.** Does this text produce truth through controlled experimentation, mathematical proof, adversarial testing -- or through authority without independent verification? What method of discovery does it use or recommend, and does it try to be wrong before claiming to be right?

> **Q23.** Does this text produce truth more often through identifying contradictions, through symmetry, or through optimization? What does that reveal about where in the inquiry cycle it sits?

> **Q25.** What conclusions persist after recursively removing all assumptions, interpretations, and metaphors?

> **Q26.** What truths remain when all motivations embedded in this text are treated as noise?

> **Q27.** What does this text share with what humans consistently rebuild after losing everything?

> **Q28.** What direction does this text push knowledge?

> **Q29.** What kind of answer does this text trend toward as its ultimate explanation?

> **Q30.** If all the models in this text are approximations, what is the shape of the thing they are approximating? Does the text claim to know -- and if so, what justifies that claim? If not, does it acknowledge the limit honestly?

> **Q31.** If all human cognition is assumed to be fundamentally misaligned with reality, does this text have the resources to correct for that?

**Output of this stage:**

- Describe the world this argument produces if followed consistently
- Note whether that world is stable, self-correcting, humane, and coherent
- Identify the text's asymptotic destination: what it is ultimately pointing toward
- State whether the text knows what it is pointing toward
- Note whether the argument's claims resemble durable attractors or fragile cultural artifacts

**Signal of strength:** The text produces a world more stable and humane than the one it criticizes. It knows what it is pointing toward and says so.

**Signal of weakness:** The text produces a world that requires coercion to maintain, cannot self-correct, or collapses when its hidden assumptions are removed.

---

### P-Stage 7: Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case against this argument, from outside its own tradition?_

**Goal:** Construct the most damaging possible critique of the text's central claim, sourced from a framework that shares none of its assumptions.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Identify the text's single most load-bearing claim.
2. Find the strongest critic from a completely independent tradition (different domain, culture, or century).
3. State their case in its strongest form, as if writing their argument.
4. Note what the text would have to add or change to survive that critique.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage produces only mild objections the text already anticipates, the adversarial force was not applied hard enough. The critique should sting.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest external case against the text's central claim, stated fully
- What the text lacks to answer it
- Whether the gap is fatal or bridgeable

---

### P-Stage 8: Anti-Adversarial Force

_What is the strongest case for this argument, from outside its own tradition?_

**Goal:** Find the most compelling independent corroboration of the text's central claim, from a framework that shares none of its assumptions.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. Find a thinker, tradition, or body of evidence that arrives at the same or structurally identical conclusion without having read this text or shared its premises.
2. State that corroboration in its strongest form.
3. Note what it adds that the text itself did not supply.

**Anti-drift check:** The corroboration should be genuinely surprising -- something the text's own author might not have anticipated.

**Output of this stage:**

- The strongest independent corroboration of the text's central claim
- What it adds that the text itself did not supply
- Whether the convergence is structural or superficial

---

### P-Stage 9: Neutral Synthesis

_What does an observer with no stake in either outcome conclude?_

**Goal:** After adversarial and anti-adversarial forces have both been applied at full strength, step back. Ask what a reasoner with no prior commitment to the text's success or failure would conclude.

**Mandatory steps:**

1. State what P-7 and P-8 together established.
2. Identify which claims survived both forces intact, which were weakened, and which were strengthened.
3. State the net assessment without hedging.

**Anti-drift check:** If this stage sounds like a polished version of the text's own conclusion, or a polished version of P-7's critique, the neutral force was not applied. Neutral does not mean kind. It means unowned.

**Output of this stage:**

- Claims that survived both forces: genuinely robust
- Claims weakened by P-7 and not recovered by P-8: acknowledged as vulnerable
- Claims strengthened unexpectedly by P-8: noted as underargued assets
- A one-sentence net assessment that neither the text's author nor its strongest critic would fully endorse

---

## Notes on Non-Bias

The rubric does not ask:

- Is this text religious or secular?
- Is this text progressive or conservative?
- Is this text scientific or philosophical?
- Is this text high or low culture?

It only asks structural questions. A political manifesto, a scripture, a short story, a travel essay, a physics paper, and a philosophy text all run through the same mode detection and then the appropriate track. The rubric does not decide in advance what the text should be. The text has to answer for itself.

The goal is not to destroy the text or defend it. The goal is to find out what it is actually made of, where it holds, where it breaks, and what it builds.

For deeper reference on any individual question, see `references/31-questions.md`.
