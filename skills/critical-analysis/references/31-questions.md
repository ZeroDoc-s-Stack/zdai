# The 31 Questions -- Full Reference

This file contains the complete 31 questions with their original answers.
These serve as the theoretical grounding for the rubric in SKILL.md.
Read this file when you need deeper context on any individual question,
or when a user asks why a particular question is structured the way it is.

---

## The Questions and Their Grounding

**Q1. What patterns remain invariant when human knowledge is translated across all symbolic systems (language, math, code, art)?**

Relation, distinction, and recursion. Every symbolic system can express "A relates to B," "A differs from B," and "this rule applies to itself." These appear in language (grammar, negation, self-reference), mathematics (functions, sets, Gödel), code (pointers, conditionals, recursion), and art (figure/ground, contrast, meta-reference). The content changes; the relational skeleton does not.

*Rubric application:* A claim that cannot survive translation into another symbolic system is culturally or linguistically dependent, not universal.

---

**Q2. What conclusions remain true if all human sensory modalities are removed and only abstract structure remains?**

Consistency constraints. The law of non-contradiction does not depend on sight or sound. Identity (A = A), implication (if P then Q), and the existence of distinction itself survive full sensory removal. These are the bones beneath perception.

*Rubric application:* Strip emotional and sensory language from a claim. What remains? If nothing remains, the claim is experiential, not structural.

---

**Q3. What ideas in human knowledge are least dependent on scale?**

Self-similarity, feedback, and conservation. The logic of "a system's output becomes its input" applies to neurons, economies, ecosystems, and galaxies. The principle that "differences drive change" holds whether describing thermodynamics or social conflict.

*Rubric application:* Test any claim at micro, macro, and conceptual levels. A claim that only holds at one scale is weaker than one that holds at all of them.

---

**Q4. If 90% of all human knowledge were false or biased, which 10% would be most recoverable from first principles?**

Logic and basic mathematics first -- they are self-certifying if you accept minimal axioms. Then basic physics (conservation laws, regularities). Then the principle that evidence constrains belief. Most history, psychology, social science, and narrative knowledge would not survive. Arithmetic and deductive inference are the most resilient.

*Rubric application:* Identify which claims in a text are first-principles recoverable and which require inherited tradition, testimony, or cultural transmission to hold.

---

**Q5. What beliefs would still emerge if each human were raised in total isolation but rediscovered knowledge from scratch?**

Causation, object permanence, self/other distinction, basic counting, fairness intuitions, some form of the golden rule. Death-awareness and some concept of the sacred appear as near-universal independent discoveries. These emerge because they are optimal responses to recurring conditions, not because they are transmitted.

*Rubric application:* Identify which claims in a text map onto these independently rediscovered beliefs. Claims that align with them have independent corroboration. Claims that contradict them carry a heavier explanatory burden.

---

**Q6. What truths require the least assumptions to hold?**

The existence of distinction (something differs from something else). From there: identity, non-contradiction, and basic logic. The closest thing to a substantive truth requiring almost no assumptions: there is structure -- not everything is undifferentiated sameness. That is a precondition for any knowledge at all.

*Rubric application:* Count the assumptions a text requires before its argument begins. Fewer is stronger. More is not automatically weaker, but each assumption must be earned.

---

**Q7. What is the smallest set of principles that can generate the largest portion of observed human knowledge?**

Strong candidates: (1) regularities exist and repeat, (2) differences have causes, (3) systems can be analyzed by their parts and interactions, (4) self-consistency is required of true claims. These four generate most of science, logic, mathematics, and practical reasoning.

*Rubric application:* Can you state the text's generative principles in four sentences or fewer? If not, the text may be less parsimonious than it appears.

---

**Q8. Which human explanations reduce the most data with the least complexity penalty?**

By minimum description length measures: calculus-based physics (Lagrangian mechanics, Maxwell's equations). In biology, natural selection. In mathematics, group theory. These are the most compression-efficient explanations humans have found.

*Rubric application:* Does this text explain a large range of phenomena with few moving parts, or does it require adding exceptions and auxiliary explanations to protect its core claim?

---

**Q9. If all human knowledge had to be encoded in the shortest possible message, what concepts would remain?**

Probably: (1) difference exists, (2) differences follow rules, (3) rules can be discovered by observation and inference, (4) apply recursively. Some argue: "there is pattern." What definitely would not survive the compression: history, narrative, most cultural knowledge, and most of what makes human life feel meaningful.

*Rubric application:* What is this text's shortest honest encoding? Is that encoding present in the text, or did you have to construct it from outside?

---

**Q10. Where do physics, mathematics, biology, and philosophy independently converge on the same structural principle without shared assumptions?**

Symmetry and its breaking. Physics: conservation laws correspond to symmetries (Noether's theorem). Mathematics: group theory is the formal study of symmetry. Biology: developmental symmetry-breaking generates form. Philosophy: identity and difference are defined by what is preserved across transformation. All four arrive at "what stays the same under change is fundamental" without borrowing from each other.

*Rubric application:* Find where the text's central claim appears independently across domains. That convergence is evidence the claim tracks something real rather than something constructed.

---

**Q11. What concepts appear simultaneously in unrelated domains with identical mathematical structure?**

Gradient descent appears in physics (least action), economics (optimization), neural networks (learning), and evolution (fitness landscapes). Power laws appear in city sizes, earthquake magnitudes, wealth distributions, word frequencies, and network connectivity. Entropy appears in thermodynamics, information theory, and ecology. These are not analogies -- the mathematics is literally identical.

*Rubric application:* When a text's core structure appears in genuinely unrelated domains without shared assumptions, that is a convergence signal. When it only appears in domains that share assumptions with the text, that is a circularity signal.

---

**Q12. What truths are rediscovered every time humans formalize a new domain?**

That the domain has limits. That there are trade-offs that cannot be simultaneously optimized. That the act of measurement interacts with the thing being measured. That the most important variables are often the hardest to quantify. Every young discipline rediscovers these.

*Rubric application:* Does this text include the limits and trade-offs it should? A text that formalizes a domain without naming its limits is either incomplete or concealing something.

---

**Q13. At what points do our most successful theories break down, and do those breakdowns share a common structure?**

They consistently break at: self-reference (Gödel, halting problem, liar's paradox), infinite regress (first cause, grounding of axioms), extreme scales (quantum gravity, aggregate vs. individual), and measurement interacting with the measured. The shared structure: a system trying to fully describe itself, or operating at its own boundary conditions, breaks down.

*Rubric application:* Find where the text's principles, applied to the text itself, break. Does the text acknowledge this? A text whose principles cannot be applied to itself without contradiction has a serious problem.

---

**Q14. What phenomena are consistently labeled paradox across disciplines, and do they point to a unified constraint?**

Self-reference (Russell, Liar, Gödel, halting), infinite regress (causation, justification, definition), emergent wholes conflicting with parts (sorites, Ship of Theseus), choice under uncertainty (St. Petersburg, Newcomb). They share a common structure: language and formal systems built for finite, non-self-referential domains generate contradictions when applied to infinity, totality, or self.

*Rubric application:* What does this text label as paradox, mystery, or exception? Do those labels cluster around a single unresolved structural problem?

---

**Q15. What is the most stable contradiction in human knowledge?**

The mind-body problem. Every proposed solution generates new problems at least as serious as those it solves. It sits at the intersection of first-person and third-person descriptions: the same reality described from inside (experience) and outside (physical process) generates a gap no current framework closes. Unlike most contradictions, this one gets harder as knowledge increases.

*Rubric application:* Find the text's equivalent of the mind-body problem -- the contradiction it keeps returning to without resolving. Is it acknowledged or papered over?

---

**Q16. If an intelligence evolved without survival pressure, what truths would it still discover?**

Logic and mathematics -- these follow from any attempt to reason consistently, not from biology. The existence of regularities. Possibly conservation (things do not come from nothing). It probably would not discover time urgency, narrative, emotion, death, or the sacred. It might discover game theory and cooperation as optimal strategies even without mortality.

*Rubric application:* Separate the text's claims into those a disembodied reasoner would arrive at independently and those that require embodiment, emotion, or evolutionary history to motivate. The second category is not necessarily wrong, but its universality is more contested.

---

**Q17. What structures in human knowledge would remain meaningful to a system with no emotions, no body, and no evolutionary history?**

Formal structure: consistency, implication, identity, distinction. Mathematical objects. The concept of optimization. Information theory and logic would survive. What probably would not mean anything: beauty, tragedy, meaning itself in the phenomenological sense, narrative, and anything that depends on caring about outcomes.

*Rubric application:* Which of the text's claims are purely structural (survive without an observer who cares) and which require an observer who already values something? Claims in the second category are not wrong, but they are observer-dependent.

---

**Q18. What would reality look like if described purely as transformations of information, without reference to objects or observers?**

Something close to where physics is moving. Wheeler's participatory universe, Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics, digital physics. No things, only differences. No states, only transitions. No location, only relative position. No time, only sequence of transformation. The paradox: this description may be unable to eliminate the observer entirely -- only relocate it.

*Rubric application:* What does this text look like when all object-references are converted to relation-references and all observer-references are made explicit? This reveals hidden dependencies.

---

**Q19. What ideas have remained stable across the longest time horizons despite changes in worldview?**

The law of non-contradiction (stable since at least Aristotle). Basic arithmetic. The golden rule (appears independently in Confucianism, Torah, New Testament, Stoicism, Hinduism, Buddhism). The idea that knowledge requires evidence. The existence of death. The idea that power corrupts. These are not theories -- they are attractors that every civilization rediscovers.

*Rubric application:* Which claims in this text have this kind of longevity? Which are recent? Recency is not a disqualifier, but it shifts the burden of proof.

---

**Q20. Which current theories are most likely to survive 1,000 years of scrutiny, based on past patterns of error?**

Strongest candidates: thermodynamics (especially entropy), Darwinian evolution (the core logic is nearly tautological), mathematical logic, general relativity at classical scales. Weakest: nutrition science, most macroeconomics, most psychiatric diagnostic categories, anything depending on our current understanding of consciousness.

*Rubric application:* Apply the same historical pattern to this text. What in it resembles thermodynamics (robust, refined by measurement, never overturned at core)? What resembles early nutrition science (confident, culturally motivated, likely to be revised)?

---

**Q21. What truths became more true as measurement improved, rather than being replaced?**

Germ theory, atomic theory, heliocentrism, the age of the universe -- all moved and narrowed by refinement, not reversal. The core claim was directionally correct; measurement just sharpened it.

*Rubric application:* Which claims in this text predict that better measurement will confirm them? Which claims require measurement to stay imprecise? The second category is fragile.

---

**Q22. What methods of discovering truth have the highest long-term success rate across domains?**

Controlled experimentation, mathematical proof, and adversarial testing (peer review, falsification attempts, red-teaming). The common structure: deliberately trying to be wrong before claiming to be right. The method that has failed most consistently: authority without evidence.

*Rubric application:* What method of discovery does this text use or recommend? Does it try to be wrong before claiming to be right? Does it rely on authority without independent verification?

---

**Q23. Does truth emerge more often from contradiction, symmetry, or optimization?**

All three, but at different stages. Contradiction reveals where old theories fail (necessary for progress). Symmetry suggests the shape of the new one. Optimization selects among alternatives. Historical pattern: contradiction breaks the old model, symmetry suggests the shape of the new one, optimization refines it.

*Rubric application:* Which of the three methods does this text primarily use? Is it appropriate to the stage of inquiry the text is at?

---

**Q24. What is the most reliable signal that a belief is false?**

It predicts observations that do not occur, while a simpler alternative predicts what does occur. More practically: a belief that cannot specify what would change the believer's mind. Unfalsifiability is the most consistent historical marker of beliefs that do not track reality. A secondary signal: a belief requiring a growing number of auxiliary hypotheses to protect it from counterevidence.

*Rubric application:* Can the text state clearly what evidence would defeat its central claim? If not, it is either unfalsifiable or its author has not thought carefully enough about its limits.

---

**Q25. What conclusions persist after recursively removing all assumptions, interpretations, and metaphors from human knowledge?**

The irreducible residue seems to be: something is, and it differs from other things. Whether that is a truth about mind, reality, or logic is itself an assumption. Descartes's project bottoms out here.

*Rubric application:* Run the recursive removal on this text. What survives? That residue is what the text is actually built on, regardless of what it claims to be built on.

---

**Q26. What truths remain when all human motivations (fear, desire, survival) are treated as noise?**

Mathematical truths, physical constants, the existence of entropy, the structure of logical implication. Evolution itself remains -- it does not care about human wishes. What disappears: almost all of ethics, political theory, economics, aesthetics, and meaning. You are left with structure without value.

*Rubric application:* Which claims in this text survive when the author's motivations (fear, desire, tribal identity, need for meaning) are removed? Claims that only survive because of motivation are not necessarily false -- but they require a different kind of justification than they typically receive.

---

**Q27. What do humans consistently rediscover after losing knowledge?**

Fire, agriculture, basic mathematics, hierarchy, trade, narrative and myth, burial of the dead, music, and justice as reciprocity. These are rediscovered because the conditions that produced them do not go away.

*Rubric application:* Which claims in this text align with what humans rebuild after collapse? Claims that align with this set have the deepest empirical grounding. Claims that contradict it are fighting against very durable human patterns.

---

**Q28. Across all domains, what direction does increasing knowledge consistently move toward?**

Simultaneously toward unity (fewer, more general principles explain more) and increased awareness of complexity (those principles generate far more phenomena than anticipated). Knowledge also consistently removes special status from the observer. The direction seems to be: more structure, less specialness.

*Rubric application:* Does this text move in that direction? Does it unify and complexify simultaneously? Or does it add specialness (to its author, tradition, or in-group) as knowledge increases?

---

**Q29. What is the asymptotic limit of explanation -- what kind of answer do all explanations trend toward?**

Formal systems tend toward either: (a) a brute fact not further explicable ("why these laws?") or (b) self-generating necessity (something that had to be this way given its own structure). Physics trends toward (a). Mathematics trends toward (b). All explanations seem to trend toward: a regularity that is simply the case, with no further explanation available or needed.

*Rubric application:* What is this text's asymptotic destination? Does it name it honestly? Does it acknowledge that the destination may be a brute fact, or does it claim to have escaped the regress?

---

**Q30. If all models are approximations, what is the shape of the thing they are approximating?**

Unknown, and possibly unknowable directly -- the models are what we have access to. What can be observed: models get better by becoming more symmetric, more general, and more information-efficient. Some argue the thing being approximated is mathematics itself (Tegmark). Others argue the shape is partly contributed by the mind doing the approximating and cannot be accessed behind it (Kant).

*Rubric application:* Does this text claim to know the shape of the thing its models are approximating? If so, what justifies that claim? If not, does it acknowledge the limit honestly?

---

**Q31. What remains true if all human cognition is fundamentally misaligned with reality?**

The claim is partially self-undermining (it is a product of human cognition). But taking it seriously: logic remains a constraint even on misaligned minds. The existence of some external reality that resists us (pain, death, failed predictions) suggests there is something cognition is misaligned with -- which is itself a truth. The structure of our errors might be the most reliable window into reality if the cognition is biased rather than random.

*Rubric application:* Does this text have the resources to correct for its own cognitive biases, or does it depend on the very cognition it cannot fully trust? Does it acknowledge this problem? Does it have an external corrective mechanism?
